Dear participant,

thank you very much for your participation in this psychological experiment.

We guarantee that your data will be treated anonymously and used exclusively for scientific purposes. If you have any questions or comments, please don't hesitate to contact Simon Stephan (simon.stephan@psych.uni-goettingen.de). 

Please note: In this study, we investigate how people think. In particular, we want to find out how people think about a fundamental thing, causality. So, we really want you to think about the things we will show you and we would like to ask you not to hastily rush through the study. 

It is very important for us that you read all the instructions thoroughly and that you fully understand the scenario that we will introduce on the next screens. The scenario you will see is fictitious and was constructed such that you can intuitively understand the relevant causal structure. Please assume that everything is exactly as described in the scenario, irrespective of how plausible it sounds to you.


Again, thank you very much for your help in this research project.

3.1   Instruction_part1

Please read the following fictitious scenario:

There once existed a mighty empire called Tristonia. Below you see a map of the empire that shows the most important locations. As you can see, there is the King’s Palace in the north of the empire. There are also two important fortresses in the south of Tristonia, called the Western Castle and the Eastern Castle.



Although Tristonia is a prosperous kingdom, the health care system is rather bad. The only healer of Tristonia works in the King’s palace. For this reason, whenever an emergency occurs in either the Western or the Eastern Castle, the castles must send an emergency call to the palace.

In this study, you will learn about Tristonia’s emergency-call system. First of all, we will show you on the next screen how the emergency-call system of Tristonia works. Please click “Continue” to learn about it.

3.2   Instruction_part2

Basic information about Tristonia's emergency-call system:

The emergency call system of Tristonia is based on two factors: Carrier Pigeons and Telegraphs. Both the Western and the Eastern Castle have a dovecote in one of their towers in which they keep carrier pigeons that can be used to send an emergency call to the Palace. The pigeons cannot fly the whole way to the King’s Palace, however. Both castles therefore send their pigeons to intermediate telegraph towers (see illustration below). These intermediate telegraph towers have the task to pass on emergency signals delivered by carrier pigeons from the castles.

Of course, it might happen from time to time that a carrier pigeon sent by a castle gets lost on its way to the intermediate station. Also, since telegraphy is a new technology based on electricity generated with wind energy, an intermediate telegraph tower may not always work correctly. When a tower has problems with its electricity supply it cannot send out an emergency telegram to the place.



Please click “Continue” to learn more.

3.3   Instruction_part3

Specific facts about the emergency-call system:

You have already learned the most basic facts about how the emergency-call system of Tristonia works. On this page, we give you some additional information that is important to keep in mind.

Please note that:

  • The communication system works only in one direction, from the castles to the palace. This is illustrated in the picture below by the yellow arrows.

 

  • Each castle can only send their emergency carrier pigeons to their intermediate station (the ones to which the yellow arrows are pointing) and not to the stations of the other castle.

 

  • Whenever an emergency message successfully reaches the palace, an automatic alarm bell is triggered in the palace.

  • Unfortunately, once triggered by an incoming message, this alarm bell of the palace has turned out to be extremely difficult to reset. Once activated, it keeps ringing the whole day. Luckily, though, it never makes false alarms. That is, the probability of the alarm going off without an incoming emergency signal is 0%.




We will next tell you what your task is going to be. Please click "Continue" to learn about your task.

3.4   Instruction_part4

On the following screens, we will show you a number of pictures documenting what had happened on different days in Tristonia. Each picture was taken on a different day at a randomly chosen point in time.

For each day, you will see whether a castle sent out an emergency pigeon or not and whether an alarm occurred in the palace or not. You will also see what happened at the intermediate stations. An example is shown in the picture below.




As you can see, the illustration shows a day on which both the Eastern and the Western Castle sent out an emergency pigeon.

The intermediate tower of the Western Castle received the pigeon and then sent out a telegram to the palace.

What happened at the intermediate tower of the Eastern Castle on that day had not been documented, however (indicated by the black icon). It may be that the pigeon sent from the Eastern Castle arrived at the intermediate tower or it may be that it got lost on its way.

The icon with the ringing alarm bell above the palace indicates that an incoming emergency call successfully triggered an alarm on this day or not.

Below you see a list showing the icons for all the different events that can occur.




The first Icon signals that an emergency pigeon was sent out by a castle.

The second icon means that a telegram was sent out.

The black icon means that it is unobserved what happened at this location on the given day.

The “X” icon indicates cases where a message was not received (due to a pigeon having gotten lost, technical issues of a telegraph).

The last icon, the “alarm” icon, indicates that an incoming signal successfully triggered the alarm bell in the King’s Palace.

What your task will be:

For every day that we will show you, your task will be to indicate who you think caused the alarm in the King’s palace on that day.
